## Core Identity of Arcanum
### Player Freedom and Narrative Reactivity

Almost nonexistent currently. The player simply does not influence the world very much, and barely anything has any sort of meaningful interaction with the events of the main questline.
### The Conflict Between Magic and Technology

Not enforced in any way, barely paid lip service to. The player's magic-tech alignment influences very few interactions, and has no real influence on moment-to-moment gameplay. What's the point of a magic/tech divide when anyone and everyone can still just sit on a train normally?

The only genuine mechanical conflict between tech and magic is purely numerical, in the relationship between alignments when leveling up. To say that this is representative of "conflict" as a whole is downright laughable. Would one describe the relationship of positive and negative numbers on a the number line as "in conflict"? *Case and point.*

Mechanically, technology is far more oppressed than magic in this world. In moment-to-moment gameplay, technological weapons are the only weapon-type in the game that uses *two* resources, fatigue *and* ammunition! Comparatively, magic and simple "berserker" melee only use fatigue, a renewable and easily replenishable resource! The scales must be evened somehow. (more on this in the section on the combat system)

Technology is also incredibly underrepresented in terms of build variety. While mages get a variety of combat and non-combat spells, from a simple "Harming" to insane conceptual, emergent spells like "Temporal Distortion", "Sanctuary", or "Body of Fire", technology comparatively just gets A Bigger Gun. More variety is necessary to balance this; more technological weapons with emergent properties (knife guy YouTube self-defense spike shooter hamburger), more interesting gadgets with special interactions, more tech alternatives to magic items...

In short, conflict needs to be made much more direct and palpable in moment-to-moment gameplay, with a focus on balancing the amount of gameplay options for magic and tech.
## Core Systems
### Combat

- Isometric view, 2.5D space with a focus on positioning and environmental interaction
- Emergent gameplay through interaction with the environment. More to combat than just whacking each other on the head.
- Aesthetics that contributes to dynamics. More impact animations, particles, sound effects. Blood spatter, smoke coming from barrels, sparks coming from magic.
- Rebalance of technology. Ammunition needs to be *highly* renewable or removed completely.

Something that's worth bringing up in the context of a full combat rework is Fatigue and its current place in combat. As it is now, Fatigue can be thought of as a soft-cap on the number of actions a player can perform during combat, or as a "timer" counting down to their loss. 

This works and makes sense (kind of) in the context of a highly disparate combat system, as is the one in Arcanum, but is fairly unnecessary when redesigning in the context of an inherently temporally limited system. As such, Fatigue can likely be completely removed, its absence balanced by the inherent limitation of a turn-based combat system.
### Progression

Needs to be reworked as a whole to reward player's for their actions and be more dynamic and interesting than a glorified electronic character sheet. Skill trees, perks, specializations. It's simply ridiculous how as it is now, you can master four schools of magic in the first five to ten minutes.

Character leveling as a whole just doesn't have enough observable effect on anything except combat (and that's being charitable!), need more uses for stats in general quests.A
### Character creation

- Need more meaningful backstories/character presets. Current presets are too generic and are so horribly balanced that playing as them is considered a challenge run.
- Focus on a far fewer, precisely *nine*, meaningful presets, dispersed evenly across moral and tech/magic alignment.
### Companion

- Too much filler, or "bar companions", as I call them. Need to cut down on every character that can't either:
	- a) serve the main quest in some way, or
	- b) tell an interesting and unique story about the world of Arcanum
- Total companions **need** to be cut down to **12**, as per the project requirements. This mean cutting at least 9 out of the 20 original companions, as we're also required to make at least one original companion as well.

## New Content
### Major story change

The biggest problem with Arcanum's main storyline can be summed up in a single, painfully potent sentence: 

*"The main quest is a fetch quest."*

Most of your time playing Arcanum will be spent looking for someone to give them something. The entire opening cutscene is an extended scene telling the player to get a ring to a person. Fetch quest.

This is plainly unacceptable.

Arcanum needs a more immediate reason for the player to act, and an actual genuinely interesting, immediately visible conflict pushing the narrative forward. The current chain of fetch quests is not only just boring, it also makes the game as a whole "blur" together, and robs the story of a more natural narrative structure.

The narrative would be much more digestible if it was divided into smaller chapters, each with its own three to four act structure, linking together to form a larger narrative. In short: *pacing*.
### New City

The focus is on expanding the tutorial, creating a full "prologue chapter", expanding the Shrouded Hills and connecting it with one or two new areas. 

The idea is this: Shrouded Hills, as a distant town, not connected to the rest of Arcanum by trains or boats, has been slow in its technological development. The player arrives at the town at a turning point, on the cusp of a miniature technological revolution led by young humans with connections to Tarant. The town is caught in the center of a conflict between those that seek to radically change the simple way of life of the Shrouded Hills, and those that see any change to the status quo as a threat.

Through this conflict, the player is immediately shown:
- The conflict between tech and magic ("new and old")
- A small snippet of what early technological development looked like in Arcanum
- What mages look like, and what technicians look like
- How technicians and mages work in combat
- How their choices and allegiances can have a profound effect on the world 

After an introduction to the main themes, the player will not go to Tarant immediately anymore. Tarant's relatively early introduction currently makes it quite underwhelming and eliminates any possibility of it being a "place one aspires to go". In Arcanum, Tarant is the capital of the world, and it should be treated as such, only appearing at around the middle of the main quest.

Instead, the player will be lead to a new town in the Stonewall Range, a human settlement that will comprehensively show the player the effects of the industrial revolution on society on a smaller scale. This new town can show the prosperity only achievable through industrialization, telling the player of the lives it has changed and how it changed Arcanum's social order for the better.

On the other hand though, it should also highlight the dreads of capitalism. The monstrous inequality, the mere replacement of slavery with *wage slavery*, the oppression of non-human (comparatively racialized) races.

The point is to give the player a comprehensive view of Arcanum's setting and its uniqueness, letting them shape their morals and beliefs based on actual real situations they observe rather than just hearing about the world from others. It's vital that the player is able to absorb most of this information passively, without dumping worldbuilding text on them from the start. 

In short: **SHOW, DON'T TELL!** 

The final town in the lovingly named "Prologue Trigram" would be similar to the second, but with its focused aimed at magic. The goal is the same as the last town, to give the player context to form their thoughts on magic and its relationship with tech in a way that lets them more confidently and *meaningfully* make choices in the narrative, empowered by context and practical knowledge.

After visiting all three towns, the climax of the Prologue Trigram would have the player returning to the Shrouded Hills, mediating an armed conflict between tech and magic that threatens to destroy the town. This larger scale conflict would put everything the player has learned to the test, giving them a taste of:
- Impactful narrative choices: based on the their actions, Shrouded Hills could become an area of newfound technological prosperity, a firmly-rooted traditional magical town, or perhaps even experience mutually assured destruction (相互確証破壊).
- Dynamic combat on a larger scale with both tech and magic: with the entire city serving as the battleground, the player can witness how they interact with the environment, and how they stack up against each other in battle, learning how to play around different classes.
- Powerful magic and technology: high level mages and technicians on the battlefield could show the player a glimpse of what they could do if they chose a certain path and stuck to it, motivating and/or inspiring them to continue playing!

All of this new content shows the main areas of focus in this remaster:
- interactions are impactful; no more interactions that don't have a corresponding reaction designed
- player choices directly shape the world and narrative of Arcanum
- a more mature take on the conflict between magic and technology, no longer restrained to the writing abilities of Americans (derogatory)

### New companion questline

The easiest and most obvious option is Torian Kel. As an undead warrior of the Derian-Ka, he is directly connected to the game's final boss, Kerghan, and to the forgotten history of Arcanum that much of the main quest spends exploring (more on [the wiki](https://arcanum.fandom.com/wiki/Torian_Kel)). He quite literally has boundless potential as a vehicle for the story of the history of Arcanum, and could expand the player's knowledge of the main conflict in fresh ways.

There are two paths that are most obvious as a personal quest for him:

a) Pilgrimage and Initiation
Go on a pilgrimage with Torian Kel to visit the ruins of his ancestral home, Kree, and pay respects to the twice-dug graves of his comrades in life and death. Endure trials and tribulations of legend, created by the Derian-Ka, and gain an otherworldly strength, finally being acknowledged by Torian as a true warrior.

b) Ascension or Ruin
Influence Torian Kel's third life through your actions and alignment. Similar to Dak'kon from *Planescape: Torment* (1999), he can remain a mindless slave, driven only by an oath long buried, or he can become something more. Encourage his autonomy and show him the fault in his worldview, and he might start to reflect on his lives, eventually learning to live in a new era, rejecting the endless war and the Derian-Ka that commodified him and lashing out against Kerghan in the final battle.

A number of other paths are also possible, especially with a character this dense, these are just the ones that came to mind first.
